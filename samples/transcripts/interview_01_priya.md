In-Depth Interview – Priya Raman

Age: 23
Generation: Gen Z
Location: Portland, OR
Occupation: Junior UX Designer
Device: Apple Watch SE
Family: Lives with two roommates

1. BACKGROUND

INTERVIEWER: Tell me a bit about how you spend your weekends.

PRIYA: Honestly? Trying to get out of the city. There's a group of us that does day hikes around Mount Hood, maybe twice a month when the weather cooperates. Nothing hardcore. I'm not summiting anything. But I like being out there and I like tracking it, seeing how far I went.

INTERVIEWER: What are you tracking it with right now?

PRIYA: Apple Watch. Which is fine for like, running around the city. But the battery is a joke. I did a six hour hike in June and it died on the way back down. I had no map, no way to call anyone. That was the moment I started looking at other watches.

2. CURRENT SOLUTION

INTERVIEWER: What did you look at?

PRIYA: Garmin, mostly. Everyone in the hiking subreddits swears by them. But I went to look at prices and it was like four hundred, five hundred dollars, and some of them were seven. For a watch. I make thirty-eight thousand dollars a year.

INTERVIEWER: So price is the barrier.

PRIYA: Price is the whole barrier. I don't need the one that does scuba diving and topographic maps of Nepal. I need battery life and a GPS that works when there's no signal. That's it. That's the entire list.

3. PAINS

INTERVIEWER: What frustrates you most about the options out there?

PRIYA: That the cheap ones are cheap in the ways that matter. I looked at some sub-two-hundred fitness bands and they're plastic, the GPS is inaccurate, and they're clearly designed for someone doing a treadmill. And the good ones cost more than my rent split. There's this huge hole in the middle where the watch I actually want should be.

INTERVIEWER: Anything else?

PRIYA: They're ugly. I'm sorry, but they are. Every serious outdoor watch looks like it was designed for a forty-five year old man doing an Ironman. I want to wear it to work on Monday. That should not be a radical request.

4. GAINS

INTERVIEWER: If you found the right watch, what would change?

PRIYA: I'd go further out. Right now I stay close to the trailhead because I don't trust my gear. If I had something with real battery life and offline maps, I'd do the longer routes. I'd probably go alone sometimes, which I don't do now.

5. OBJECTIONS

INTERVIEWER: What would make you hesitate to buy a cheaper Garmin?

PRIYA: I'd assume they cut something important. Like, what's the catch? If it's three hundred and the other one's six, what did they take out? I'd want to see the actual spec comparison, not marketing. And I'd want to know the GPS is the same chip, not a downgraded one.

INTERVIEWER: What would change your mind?

PRIYA: Someone I trust wearing it for six months and saying the battery held up. Reviews from people who actually hike, not tech reviewers who wore it to the gym twice.

6. BRAND PERCEPTION

INTERVIEWER: What do you think of Garmin as a brand?

PRIYA: Competent. Boring. Trustworthy in a dad kind of way. I don't think of them as being for me, but I don't think of them as bad. If they made something that looked like it was designed this decade I'd pay attention.

7. VOCABULARY

INTERVIEWER: How do you describe what you're looking for to friends?

PRIYA: I say I want a "real" watch that doesn't cost a paycheck. Or I say I want something that "won't die on me." Battery anxiety is the phrase I use most, actually. That's the feeling.
